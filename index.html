The Prologue

I came for the marketing.
I stayed for the data.

I started in computer science, curious about how technology and people connect. My first real job put me inside a marketing team — and I fell in love with something I didn't expect: the numbers underneath every decision. I was more interested in understanding why the numbers moved than actually moving them.

Every campaign had data. Every click was a signal. Every dashboard reflected real user behavior. I realized I was spending more time asking why the metrics moved than anything else — and honestly, I didn't want to stop.

Marketing gave me something most data analysts don't have — I learned how businesses think, how customers feel, and how decisions actually get made in real organizations. I sat in rooms with CEOs and COOs, presented findings, and learned to make complex things simple for people who needed answers fast.

Then I started learning Python. Then SQL. Then Power BI. Not because someone told me to — because the questions I was encountering at work were getting bigger than Excel could answer. And I wanted to keep up with my own curiosity.

"Marketing showed me the door. Analytics showed me the castle behind it."

Now I build projects about why humans do what they do. I analyze real data about real people. And I'm looking for a team where that kind of curiosity is actually useful.
